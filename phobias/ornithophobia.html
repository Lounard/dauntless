Ornithophobia

Ornithophobia is the irrational fear of birds. The meaning of the word comes from Greek origins, 'Ornitho' translates to bird and 'phobos', means to dread or to fear. A person with Ornithophobia might be afraid of all birds or a particular bird or types of similar birds. For example, the bird phobia might only be based around pigeons, birds of prey, parrots, etc. A person who has this fear will avoid birds at all costs and could have a panic attack at even the sight of just a feather.

Causes of Ornithphobia

Though it can be developed at any age due to trauma, Ornithophobia is usually developed in childhood. Children can be easily intimidated by aggressive geese, or the flapping wings of pigeons that fly close to their faces. Even a pet parrot may have given a nasty nip. It can equally be learned from a parent or sibling who has Ornithophobia.

Causes of Ornithophobia

Children learn very quickly and closely watch how the people around them react to different stimuli. If the people closest to them show that birds are something to be feared, a child will easily pick the phobia up.

Causes of Ornithophobia

Young children are also very vulnerable to developing fears through media and film. Birds portrayed as evil and dangerous in films like Alfred Hitchcock's The Birds can contribute to one developing Ornithophobia. Wildlife documentaries that show predatory or scavenging birds eating pray could also create the phobia in a young child.

Symptoms of Ornithophobia

Like many other phobias, people who suffer from Ornithophobia can experience a range of different emotional and physical symptoms. Seeing a bird could cause one with Ornithophobia to scream and react in a fight or flight response by either running away or lashing out trying to 'protect themselves' in their minds from the birds.

Rapid heart rate

A rapid or fast heartbeat is when your heart is beating faster than normal. A normal heart rate is 60 to 100 beats per minute. Tachycardia is considered a heart rate of greater than 100 beats per minute. If you are exercising, or performing any kind of activity, your heart will normally beat faster.

Dizziness

Dizziness is a term used to describe a range of sensations, such as feeling faint, woozy, weak or unsteady. Dizziness that creates the false sense that you or your surroundings are spinning or moving is called vertigo.

Hot or cold flashes

Hot flashes are sudden feelings of warmth, which are usually most intense over the face, neck and chest. Your skin might redden, as if you're blushing. Hot flashes can also cause sweating, and if you lose too much body heat, you might feel chilled afterward. Cold flashes, which are related to hot flashes in some cases, may be less familiar. A cold flash is a tingling, shivery, cold feeling that can suddenly come over your body.

Hysteria

Hysteria colloquially means ungovernable emotional excess. Generally, modern medical professionals have abandoned using the term "hysteria" to denote a diagnostic category, replacing it with more precisely defined categories, such as somatization disorder.

Chest pain

Chest pain is discomfort, typically in the front of the chest. It may be described as sharp, dull, pressure, heaviness, or squeezing. It can be divided into heart-related and non heart related pain. Pain due to insufficient blood flow to the heart is also called angina pectoris.

Feeling of choking

Choking is a blockage of the upper airway by food or other objects, which prevents a person from breathing effectively. Choking can cause a simple coughing fit, but complete blockage of the airway may lead to death.

Schizophrenic

Schizophrenia is a disabling brain disorder that affects the way your brain works and how you think. Schizophrenics can't always tell reality from fantasy.

Trembling and/or sweating

Trembling is shaking or quivering, typically as a result of anxiety, excitement, or frailty. This scenario can be happen at the same time while sweating.

Numbness or tingling sensation

Numbness describes a loss of sensation or feeling in a part of your body. Numbness can occur along a single nerve on one side of the body, or it may occur symmetrically, on both sides of the body.
