Mastigophobia

Mastigophobia is the fear of punishment. The origin of the word mastigo is Greek (meaning whip or flog) and phobia is Greek (meaning fear). Mastigophobia is considered to be a specific phobia. It is generally accepted that phobias arise from a combination of external events (i.e. traumatic events) and internal predispositions (i.e. heredity or genetics)

  • Learn More

Causes of Mastigophobia

Traumatic Event. A traumatic or negative experience can trigger the development of specific phobias. Many specific phobias can be traced back to a specific triggering event, usually a traumatic experience at an early age. A punishment from doing something wrong can result to this phobia

Causes of Mastigophobia

Genetics. It is believed that heredity, genetics, and brain chemistry combine with life-experiences to play a major role in the development of phobias.

Causes of Mastigophobia

Traumatic brain injury. Brain injuries, such as concussions, have been linked to the development of anxiety disorders. A brain injury appears to increase fear conditioning and predispose the brain to fear learning during stressful events experienced after the injury.

Symptoms of Mastigophobia

As with any phobia, the symptoms vary by person depending on their level of fear. People that suffer from Mastigophobia, which is a Specific phobia, try to avoid not only the exact objects or situations that trigger it but sometimes in severe cases the thought of those thing all together.

Immediate feelings of anxiety when thinking about punishments

A person doesn’t necessarily need to be in a situation exposed to punishment to experience Mastigophobia.

Ringing in your ears
