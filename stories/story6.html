I am mentally ill, and this is my story

By Katherine Lopez

It was a sunny Wednesday afternoon in 2014 when I learned that I had anxiety and depression. I went to see a psychologist at her clinic at UP Diliman. After an hour of sobbing and telling my story, she diagnosed me with anxiety and depression. And because my episodes had been going on for so long, she felt medications were necessary. She asked me to come back for another session, and she would then refer me to a psychiatrist. I thanked her, paid her secretary, and left her clinic.

I did not come back for a follow-up, despite text messages from her secretary asking me when I would schedule my next visit.

At that time, I thought I could overcome it by thinking happy thoughts or keeping myself busy. But I was wrong, just like countless people who think that mental health matters can be dealt with by sheer will power. Anxiety and depression are mental illnesses and, like other physical illnesses, they need treatment.

Ever since I was a child, I had always been emotional. People have drummed into my head that I am oversensitive, or maramdamin, and I believed them. I would cry easily whenever my parents reprimanded me. Every child gets scolded by their parents from time to time. But with me, it was different. Long after the scolding was done, what I did still stayed with me. I felt I was the worst child in the world.

At school, it was the same. Misunderstandings or fights with classmates stayed in my heart for a very long time. And the results were damaging: a distorted self-image, irrational fear, unexplained sadness, and the belief that I never seem to do things right. All these pierced a hole in my heart, a wound that never healed even now that I am already an adult. Little did I know that this was already a symptom of a mental illness.

A year after my visit to the psychologist at UP Diliman, I went to see a psychiatrist. Let’s call him Dr. B. I did not know this doctor—no one referred me to him—but I mustered all my courage to see him. And just like my visit to the psychologist, I started the session by telling him my story.

I told him I was having trouble sleeping at night. My mind would race with so many thoughts, but I couldn’t put a finger on any of them. I also told him that sad thoughts often occupied my mind, that I was constantly afraid of what people would think of me. I didn’t want to be despised, rejected, or disliked.

It was as if I squeezed my life story in an hour of talking. Dr. B diagnosed me with depression, generalized anxiety disorder, and social anxiety disorder. All three sounded big to me. He prescribed me with an antidepressant (Escitalopram) and an anxiolytic (Alprazolam) to help me with my symptoms.

I saw Dr. B once a month and, during every session, I would tell him about my episodes. My insecurities caused a lot of these episodes, as well as my constant fear of people judging me. Sad thoughts still occupied my mind before I slept even if there was no specific reason. He then told me that I was showing symptoms of bipolar disorder. He added an antipsychotic (Quetiapine) to the medications I was taking. My dosage got adjusted every time I saw him, but the episodes kept on coming back.

In February 2016, Dr. B decided that I needed more than medicines. He recommended that I see a psychiatrist who does cognitive behavior therapy. That was how I got to know Dr. G, my current psychiatrist.

Cognitive behavior therapy gave me the help that my medicines alone could not give. It taught me to overcome my negative, and oftentimes destructive, thinking patterns. My most destructive thought comes every time I feel insecure. I feel that I am not good enough, and that people around me are evaluating me using an invisible standard. I put certain people on a pedestal and think that I am nothing compared with them and with what they have accomplished.

It is easy to say that I should stop comparing myself with other people. But this is not plain jealousy or envy—this is one of the effects of my illness. It distorts the way I see things. Dr. G keeps on telling me that I am not my thoughts, and my thoughts are not necessarily real. I am not making them up, but it is my illness doing the thinking, so I arrive at irrational conclusions.

One of the exercises that Dr. G taught me is this: Every time a negative thought comes up, I must look for concrete proof to support it. If I don’t find any proof, then it is simply an irrational thought messing with me. For example, whenever I feel small compared with someone else, I must ask myself these: 1) Is there any evidence that contradicts this thought?; 2) Can I identify unhealthy patterns of thinking; and 3) What would I say to a friend who is having the same thought?

This process helps me arrive at a positive and balanced thought. But sometimes, I do not succeed. There are days I am on a dip and I cannot do anything right. Nothing can lift my spirits. I just want to stay in bed all day and not move (literally!). I don’t even have the energy to eat. I don’t want to go out because I feel that the moment I step out of our front door, a series of unfortunate events is bound to greet me.

This is what mental illness can do. It is a never-ending cycle. Diagnosis can even change from time to time. After a year of therapy with Dr. G, she diagnosed me with major depressive disorder. Symptoms of different mental illnesses overlap. Major depressive disorder and bipolar disorder both have depressive states. Anxiety and depression often come in tandem.

But these do not mean that the battle against mental illness cannot be won. With professional help, symptoms can be managed. Seeing a psychiatrist is one of the best decisions I have ever made in my life. Every session is costly, and the medicines are just as expensive. I have devised every way possible so I can cover these expenses. It is a struggle, but I always tell myself: Money will come. I need to get better.

Looking back on that afternoon when I saw a psychologist, I can say that I have greatly improved. I can now sleep at night at a decent time. I know when an episode is imminent, so I give myself a break. I know when I am on a dip, so I become gentle with myself. Having a mental illness also taught me to be more understanding and considerate, because the person beside me might be having the same struggle as I am. Dark clouds may often surround me, but as long as I follow my psychiatrist’s advice, I know I will see rays of sun, too.
